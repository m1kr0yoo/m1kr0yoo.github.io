The "North Central" Dialect

Shared by:

  1. North Dakota
  2. South Dakota
  3. Minnesota
  4. Northern Wisconsin

Canadian English

You can hear Canadian English in Northern Wisconsin through the use of Canadian Raising.

Aileen Schneider of Marburg University in Germany displays this phenonomenon in her video lecture "Canadian Raising Linguistics Lecture".

  • The dipthongs "aj" (pronounced "EYE") & "aw" (pronounced "AY") are open vowels
    • "Rider" and "writer" sound the same

Upper Peninsula English

Due to the proximity of Michigan's upper peninsula, many northern Wisconsinites share smmilar linguistic features to the UP.

Sarah Braun in “Mapping Perceptions of Language Variation in Wisconsin” references the work of Salmons and Purnell by noting the use of UP English by the north is highlighted by three features:

  1. Interdental fricative stopping, modifying the "th" sound
    1. Words such as "think" become "dink"
  2. The use of "eh"
    1. Similar to the Canadian stereotype, i.e. "You grabbed lunch, eh?"
  3. Clipping "ing"
    1. Braun gives the example "goin' fishin'"

The "Upper Midlands" Dialect

Spoken throughout the eastern and central United States, including southern Wisconsin.

Cornish Influence

Kristen Speth in the book Wisconsin Talks explains that immigrants from Cornwall arrived to Wisconsin between 1830 and 1850, bringing their dialect with them.

Some examples of Cornish English in Wisconsin include:

  • The word "thee" was used instead of "them"
  • "House" is pronounced like "ouse"
  • The word "catched" was used instead of "caught"

Illinois ties

Similar to the influence of Upper Peninsula English to the "North Central" dialect, the "Upper Midlands" dialect is influenced by its proximity to Illinois.

This is due to the development of transportation, which opened up trade between Wisconsinites and Illinoisans as noted by Kirstin Speth.

Speth provides many examples of this influence:

  • Southern Wisconsinites may pronounce many words like Illinoisans, including "Saturdee" or "warsh".
  • The low vowel "l" has changed, so words such as "cold" sound like "coed" or "code".
  • The "t" is also shifted within many phrases, such as "a t'all" for "at all".
